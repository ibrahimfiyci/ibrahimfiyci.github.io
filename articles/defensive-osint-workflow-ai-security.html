Building a Defensive OSINT Workflow for AI Security

By James Fiyci | Updated July 10, 2026

OSINT is most valuable when it turns public information into useful decisions. For AI security, that can mean tracking public advisories, exposed AI services, vendor updates, and research that changes how teams should secure model-facing applications.

Inputs

A defensive workflow can monitor official bulletins, vendor security pages, vulnerability feeds, AI security framework updates, and project release notes.

Normalization

Each item should be tagged with source, date, affected technology, severity, confidence, and relevance to AI systems such as agents, retrieval pipelines, model APIs, or prompt-processing workflows.

Output

The final product should be a short briefing: what happened, why it matters, what assets may be affected, and what a defender should check next.

Safety boundary

This workflow stays defensive. It uses public information, avoids intrusive collection, and focuses on risk awareness, not exploitation.
